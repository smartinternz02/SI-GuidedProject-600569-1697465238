Meet Our Model's Recognised Felis Species

Domestic Cat

Felis catus

Domestic cats, scientifically referred to as Felis catus, are beloved companions known for their diverse breeds, colors, and personalities. They have a long history of living alongside humans, combining independence with affection.

Habitat: Domestic cats live in a wide range of environments worldwide, from urban areas to rural settings.

Size: Domestic cats come in various sizes, depending on their breed.
Coat: The coat of domestic cats varies greatly, with a wide range of colors, patterns, and lengths.
Ears: Ears can be upright or folded, depending on the breed.

Conservation Status: Not applicable (due to their domestication)

African Wildcat

Felis lybica

The African Wildcat, scientifically known as Felis lybica, is a small, slender cat with a sandy coat adorned with subtle stripes and spots. These skilled hunters are known for their adaptability, allowing them to thrive in diverse African habitats.

Habitat: African Wildcats are versatile, inhabiting various environments across Africa, from savannas and deserts to forests.

Size: African Wildcats are small to medium-sized cats, with males typically larger than females. Coat: They have a short, sandy-colored coat adorned with faint stripes and spots. Ears: Their ears are relatively short compared to some other wildcats.

Conservation Status: Least Concern

Chinese Mountain Cat

Felis bieti

The Chinese Mountain Cat, or Felis bieti, thrives in the challenging mountainous environments of China. Their dense fur and unique appearance help them adapt to harsh conditions. They are solitary and elusive hunters.

Habitat: Chinese Mountain Cats are native to high-altitude regions of China.

Size: Chinese Mountain Cats are small cats, with stocky bodies. Coat: Their fur is thick and soft, often grayish or pale brown with dark spots. Ears: They have distinctive, rounded ears with small tufts of hair.

Conservation Status: Vulnerable

European Wild Cat

Felis silvestris silvestris

The European Wildcat, or Felis silvestris silvestris, closely resembles domestic cats but retains its wild instincts. They are known for their solitary nature and excellent hunting skills.

Habitat: European Wildcats inhabit forests and woodlands in Europe.

Size: European Wildcats closely resemble domestic cats in size. Coat: Their fur is dense and often grayish with a striped pattern. Ears: They have typical cat ears with a small amount of tufting.

Conservation Status: Least Concern

Jungle Cat

Felis chaus

The Jungle Cat, scientifically known as Felis chaus, is a medium-sized wildcat known for its agility and hunting prowess. They are skilled hunters that adapt to their diverse habitats.

Habitat: Jungle Cats thrive in wetlands, grassy areas, and forests in regions including Asia and the Middle East.

Size: Jungle Cats are medium-sized cats with a slender build. Coat: Their coat is usually tawny or grayish with dark spots and stripes. Ears: They have relatively long and pointed ears.

Conservation Status: Least Concern

Sand Cat

Felis margarita

The Sand Cat, or Felis margarita, is uniquely adapted to thrive in harsh desert conditions. Their large ears help with heat regulation, and they are efficient hunters in challenging environments.

Habitat: Sand Cats are specialized for life in arid desert environments.

Size: Sand Cats are small cats with a compact and stocky body. Coat: They have a pale, sand-colored coat, perfectly suited to their desert habitat. Ears: Their most distinctive feature is their large, prominent ears.

Conservation Status: Least Concern
